Pure-PHP HTTP/3: status and known bottlenecks. Captured at the end of the H3-prep work series (phases through 15). Two things are recorded here: the decision to defer full 0-RTT session resumption, and a measured list of where the pure-PHP HTTP/3 listener spends its time relative to the HTTP/2 implementation. This is a planning document for the next round of work, not a final spec.

0-RTT (RFC 9001 §4.6) — deferred

Status: the constants and extension IDs are in place (HS_NEW_SESSION_TICKET, HS_END_OF_EARLY_DATA, EXT_PRE_SHARED_KEY, EXT_EARLY_DATA, EXT_PSK_KEY_EXCHANGE_MODES). Nothing else is wired.

Why it didn't ship in this round: 0-RTT touches more surface than any of phases 12–15 combined, and the parts that are easy to get wrong (PSK binder math, anti-replay accounting, ticket key rotation) are also the parts that, when wrong, fail silently or accept replay attacks. It is better to leave the feature off than to claim partial support.

Concretely, a working 0-RTT implementation needs all of:

  1. NewSessionTicket emission. RFC 8446 §4.6.1 message format; lives in the encrypted handshake (an application_data record after Finished). Carries ticket_lifetime, ticket_age_add, ticket_nonce, the ticket bytes, and a quic_transport_parameters extension carrying the server's max_early_data_size plus the original transport params (RFC 9001 §7.4.1).
  2. Stateless ticket encryption with a server STEK (Session Ticket Encryption Key). AES-GCM is fine and atto already has it. The ticket payload should bind the ALPN, the cipher suite, and the resumption_master_secret from the original handshake. Stateful tickets are simpler but tie a ticket to one server process; for a single-process atto deployment that is acceptable too.
  3. ClientHello PSK extension parsing on resumption. RFC 8446 §4.2.11: psk_key_exchange_modes extension plus the PSK identity list plus binder MACs over the truncated ClientHello transcript hash. The transcript boundary for the binder is the tricky part: it ends just before the binder list itself, needing a custom hash boundary that does not align with any other transcript boundary the engine currently computes.
  4. Binder verification. HMAC of the truncated transcript using the binder_key derived from the resumption secret. If verification fails, fall back to a normal handshake rather than erroring — the spec permits server-side rejection of any PSK.
  5. New encryption level LEVEL_EARLY_DATA plus the 0-RTT key schedule per RFC 9001 §5.5. 0-RTT keys derive from client_early_traffic_secret, which itself derives from the resumption master secret (RFC 8446 §7.1). This is a new QuicPacketKeys::fromTrafficSecret usage with a different label.
  6. 0-RTT packet decoding. Long-header type 0b01. Same packet-number space as 1-RTT (per RFC 9001 §17.2.3, 0-RTT and 1-RTT share PNs after handshake completion; the server discards 0-RTT keys at that point per §5.5).
  7. Anti-replay buffer. A bounded LRU keyed on (ticket_id, PN) or on a token nonce in the ticket. RFC 9001 §9.2: the server must detect replays within the window the ticket allows. atto's single-process model makes this easier than a distributed server, but the buffer still needs reasonable sizing and a ticket-validity timeout.
  8. EncryptedExtensions handling: include or omit the early_data extension based on whether 0-RTT was accepted; the client uses that signal to know whether to expect 1-RTT to promote its already-sent 0-RTT data or to retransmit it as 1-RTT.

The quick parts (1, 2, 6) are a few hundred lines. The TLS-side PSK and binder math (3, 4, 5) plus the anti-replay buffer (7) are the bulk of the work, and exactly where correctness matters. A future pass should plan a multi-session scope rather than trying to land it in one go.

For now, atto's HTTP/3 server completes a full 1-RTT handshake on every new connection. Connection mobility (phase 15) and stateless reset (phase 14) cover the operational pain 0-RTT would also have addressed; the remaining gap is purely the handshake-RTT saving on resumed connections.

Bottlenecks: where pure-PHP HTTP/3 spends time vs HTTP/2

Measurements taken on the dev sandbox over loopback. The bench command is examples/18 HTTP Benchmarks/bench.php with --skip filtered to the case under analysis. Wall-time numbers are medians over 10 iterations of /big (1 MiB body) unless noted.

PathMedian
HTTP/2 over loopback~8 ms
HTTP/3 (pure-PHP) loopback~100 ms (12x slower)

The ratio narrows on real RTT (5–10 ms each way) because H3's 0-RTT-after-resumption advantage shows there, and because H2's TCP head-of-line blocking starts to bite. On loopback it is a pure PHP-side processing comparison.

Per-method bottleneck list, ordered by total cost:

  1. AEAD (encrypt/decrypt + header protection) — ~36 ms (~36% of /big). QuicPacketKeys::seal ~4.2 us/call, n~4880, ~21 ms; headerProtectionMask ~2.6 us/call, n~5161, ~13 ms; open ~8 us/call, n~281, ~2 ms. Each /big response goes through ~950 packets, each getting one seal, one HP mask, and over the connection lifetime a handful of opens for incoming ACKs. The seal cost is openssl's per-call dispatch overhead, not the cipher math — openssl_encrypt at 1100 bytes is 2 us for AES-128-GCM but the PHP→openssl roundtrip adds another 2 us per call. Helpful improvements: batch ~16 HP sample-blocks into one openssl call and slice the output (~6 ms, halves HP cost); sodium for AES-GCM is not available (sodium only exposes AES-256-GCM, atto needs 128), though ChaCha20-Poly1305 already uses the faster sodium path; PHP 8.4's openssl functions are slightly faster and mostly already captured.
  2. Frame encode/decode dispatch — ~5–8 ms. QuicFrame::encode (~5000 calls per /big) dispatches via a switch on type and concatenates a few varints plus the payload; the hot case encodeStream could be inlined into flushStreams to skip the case lookup, saving ~1 us per packet. decodeAll runs ~30 times per /big (ACKs only).
  3. Per-packet emit() loop bookkeeping — ~10–15 ms. QuicConnection::emit walks send_queue, classifies the payload, increments PN, builds a QuicPacket, calls encodeShort, appends to sent_packets, and bumps bytes_in_flight. For 950 packets that is many array operations (PHP array writes cost 100–200 ns each; the inner loop touches ~5 arrays per iteration). Wins: pool QuicPacket objects instead of allocating fresh per packet (~0.5 ms); pack the sent_packets entry into a string/array instead of an associative array (~1 ms); hoist the next_pn[$level]++ deref outside the loop where missed.
  4. Stream send-buffer chunking — ~8–12 ms. For /big the body is one 1 MiB string in QuicStream::send_buf; flushStreams takeForFrame()s 1100-byte chunks repeatedly, each a substr() of the remaining buffer. substr on a 1 MiB string is O(N) because it allocates a new buffer; over 950 chunks that is ~480 MiB of allocator churn for a 1 MiB transfer. Best fix: keep an integer offset into send_buf instead of mutating it — takeForFrame returns substr($buf, $offset, $chunk) and offset advances; send_buf stays immutable until the transfer completes (~5 ms).
  5. processDatagram per-packet path — ~3–5 ms (inbound only). Most /big load is outbound, so this cost is dominated by the inbound ACK stream (~30 ACK packets per 1 MiB response). Each ACK runs decodeShort + AEAD open + frame decode + processAck; processAck walks ACK ranges, which is cheap for the small ACK packets aioquic sends.
  6. Loss-detection timer arithmetic — ~2–3 ms. setLossDetectionTimer (called on every PN change to sent_packets) walks loss_time across all three PN spaces and sent_packets to find the earliest deadline. Could cache the earliest entry and invalidate only on insert/remove of packets earlier than the cached value (~1–2 ms).
  7. UDP send syscall overhead — ~10 ms. stream_socket_sendto once per emitted packet (~950 calls per /big). Could batch via sendmmsg if PHP exposed it (it doesn't). On Linux, sendto for a 1200-byte UDP packet is ~10 us. The real fix is at the PHP/libc level, not actionable from inside atto today.
  8. Header protection ChaCha20 openssl vs sodium-XOR. Already on the openssl-or-sodium fallback path; openssl is used when available (the faster of the two). On macOS + LibreSSL the pure-PHP RFC 8439 fallback fires and is significantly slower — mostly a packaging note, not a code fix.

H2 over the same /big bench is ~8 ms total because HTTP/2 framing is much simpler (one HEADERS + one DATA frame with a length prefix versus ~950 QUIC packets each with AEAD); TLS wrapping happens inside openssl in one go via stream filters rather than per-record from PHP; and there is no per-packet PN bookkeeping, no congestion control inside PHP, and no header protection.

Realistic future-perf ceiling

Most of the H2/H3 gap is intrinsic to the design choice: H3 does in PHP what H2 lets the kernel and openssl do in C. The biggest realizable wins from inside the H3 listener:

  • flushStreams substr chunking (~5 ms saved) — easy
  • loss-timer caching (~1–2 ms saved) — easy
  • HP mask batching (~6 ms saved) — medium
  • pool / streamline emit() per-packet allocations — medium
  • sent_packets entries packed instead of associative — medium

If all landed cleanly, /big would drop from ~100 ms to ~80 ms — still ~10x slower than H2. To get materially closer, the path forward is the libquiche-FFI listener (H3QuicheListener), which delegates the hot loop to native code. But on this dev box even libquiche came in slower than the pure-PHP listener for /big (the FFI roundtrip per call dominates), which is the surprise that motivated keeping H3Listener as primary.

For deployments where H3 throughput matters more than the joys of a single-file framework, the real answer is a frontend reverse proxy (nginx, caddy, h2o) speaking H3 to clients and H2 to atto. That puts atto in the role where its measured strengths show: expressive routing, low-overhead H2/H1 dispatch, and a single PHP file you can read end to end.
